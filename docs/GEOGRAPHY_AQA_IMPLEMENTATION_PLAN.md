# AQA GCSE Geography (8035) — Full Implementation Plan

**Purpose:** Make learning effective and engaging so students cover all test topics and build the knowledge, understanding, evaluation, and skills required by AQA assessment objectives.  
**Specification:** AQA GCSE Geography 8035 (linear; three papers; 35% + 35% + 30%).  
**Date:** February 2025.

---

## 1. AQA Geography Topic List (Summary)

Content is organised into **four units**, with **option choices** in two units:

| Unit | Paper | Weight | Content | Options |
|------|-------|--------|---------|---------|
| **3.1 Living with the physical environment** | Paper 1 | 35% | Section A: Natural hazards (all); Section B: Living world (Ecosystems + Rainforests + **one** from Desert or Cold); Section C: UK physical landscapes (overview + **two** from Coastal, River, Glacial) | Living world: Hot deserts **or** Cold environments; Landscapes: choose **two** from Coastal, River, Glacial |
| **3.2 Challenges in the human environment** | Paper 2 | 35% | Section A: Urban issues (all); Section B: Changing economic world (all); Section C: Resource management (overview + **one** from Food, Water, Energy) | Resource: Food **or** Water **or** Energy |
| **3.3 Geographical applications** | Paper 3 | 30% | Section A: Issue evaluation (pre-release); Section B: Fieldwork (two enquiries, contrasting environments, physical + human) | — |
| **3.4 Geographical skills** | All papers | — | Cartographic, graphical, numerical, statistical, enquiry, literacy | — |

**Assessment structure:**
- **Paper 1:** Living with the physical environment — 1 hr 30 min, 88 marks.
- **Paper 2:** Challenges in the human environment — 1 hr 30 min, 88 marks.
- **Paper 3:** Geographical applications — 1 hr 30 min, 76 marks (issue evaluation + fieldwork questions).

**Assessment objectives (approx.):** AO1 knowledge 15%, AO2 understanding 25%, AO3 apply/interpret/analyse/evaluate 35% (incl. 10% fieldwork), AO4 skills 25% (incl. 5% fieldwork). SPaG assessed in extended responses.

**Case studies vs examples:** AQA distinguishes **case studies** (broader, greater depth) from **examples** (focused, smaller scale). Both require named locations.

## 2. Full AQA Topic List (By Section)

### 2.1 Paper 1 — Living with the physical environment

#### Section A: The challenge of natural hazards (all required)

**3.1.1.1 Natural hazards**
- Definition; types of natural hazard; factors affecting hazard risk.

**3.1.1.2 Tectonic hazards**
- Plate tectonics theory; global distribution of earthquakes/volcanoes; physical processes at constructive, destructive, conservative margins; primary/secondary effects; immediate/long-term responses; **named examples** in contrasting wealth (HIC vs LIC/NEE); why people live in areas at risk; monitoring, prediction, protection, planning.

**3.1.1.3 Weather hazards**
- General atmospheric circulation; pressure belts and surface winds; global distribution of tropical storms; causes and formation; structure and features; climate change effects; effects and responses; **named example** of tropical storm; UK weather hazards overview; **example** of UK extreme weather event (causes, impacts, management); evidence of UK weather extremes.

**3.1.1.4 Climate change**
- Evidence from Quaternary to present; natural causes (orbital, volcanic, solar); human causes (fossil fuels, agriculture, deforestation); effects; mitigation (alternative energy, carbon capture, afforestation, international agreements); adaptation (agriculture, water supply, sea-level risk).

---

#### Section B: The living world (mandatory + one choice)

**3.1.2.1 Ecosystems** (required)
- Small-scale UK ecosystem **example** (interrelationships, producers/consumers/decomposers, food chain, food web, nutrient cycling); balance; impact of changing one component; overview of large-scale global ecosystems.

**3.1.2.2 Tropical rainforests** (required)
- Physical characteristics; interdependence of climate, water, soils, plants, animals, people; adaptations; biodiversity; deforestation rates; **case study** (causes: subsistence/commercial farming, logging, roads, minerals, energy, settlement, population; impacts: economic, soil erosion, climate); sustainable management (selective logging, conservation, ecotourism, international agreements, debt reduction).

**3.1.2.3 Hot deserts** (choose one with 3.1.2.4)
- Physical characteristics; interdependence; adaptations; biodiversity; **case study** (development opportunities: minerals, energy, farming, tourism; challenges: temperature, water, inaccessibility); desertification (causes: climate change, population, fuel wood, overgrazing, over-cultivation and soil erosion; strategies: water/soil management, tree planting, technology).

**3.1.2.4 Cold environments** (choose one with 3.1.2.3)
- Physical characteristics; interdependence of climate, permafrost, soils, plants, animals; adaptations; biodiversity; **case study** (opportunities: minerals, energy, fishing, tourism; challenges: temperature, inaccessibility, buildings, infrastructure); wilderness value; conservation; strategies (technology, governments, international agreements).

---

#### Section C: Physical landscapes in the UK (overview + two choices)

**3.1.3.1 UK physical landscapes** (required)
- Overview of major upland/lowland areas and river systems.

**3.1.3.2 Coastal landscapes** (choose two with 3.1.3.3, 3.1.3.4)
- Wave types; weathering (mechanical, chemical); mass movement (sliding, slumping, rock falls); erosion (hydraulic action, abrasion, attrition); transportation (longshore drift); deposition; geological structure and rock type; landforms of erosion (headlands/bays, cliffs/wave-cut platforms, caves/arches/stacks); landforms of deposition (beaches, dunes, spits, bars); **example** UK coastline; hard engineering (sea walls, rock armour, gabions, groynes); soft engineering (beach nourishment, dune regeneration); managed retreat; **example** of UK coastal management scheme.

**3.1.3.3 River landscapes** (choose two with 3.1.3.2, 3.1.3.4)
- Long profile; cross profile; fluvial erosion/transportation/deposition; landforms of erosion (interlocking spurs, waterfalls, gorges); erosion + deposition (meanders, ox-bow lakes); deposition (levées, flood plains, estuaries); **example** UK river valley; physical/human factors affecting flood risk; hydrographs; hard engineering (dams, straightening, embankments, flood relief channels); soft engineering (flood warnings, flood plain zoning, tree planting, river restoration); **example** UK flood management scheme.

**3.1.3.4 Glacial landscapes** (choose two with 3.1.3.2, 3.1.3.3)
- Maximum ice extent; glacial processes (freeze-thaw, abrasion, plucking, rotational slip, bulldozing, till, outwash); landforms of erosion (corries, arêtes, pyramidal peaks, truncated spurs, troughs, ribbon lakes, hanging valleys); landforms of deposition (erratics, drumlins, moraines); **example** UK glaciated upland; economic activities (tourism, farming, forestry, quarrying); conflicts; **example** glaciated upland tourism (attractions, impacts, management).

### 2.2 Paper 2 — Challenges in the human environment

#### Section A: Urban issues and challenges (all required)

- Global pattern of urban change; urban trends in HICs vs LICs; factors (migration push–pull, natural increase); megacities.
- **Case study:** major city in LIC/NEE — location, importance, causes of growth, opportunities (social: health, education, water, energy; economic: industrial stimulus), challenges (slums, water, sanitation, energy, services, unemployment, crime, waste, pollution, traffic); **example** of urban planning improving quality of life.
- UK population distribution; major cities.
- **Case study:** major city in UK — location, importance, migration impacts, opportunities (cultural mix, recreation, employment, transport, urban greening), challenges (deprivation, inequality, housing, education, health, employment, dereliction, brownfield/greenfield, waste, sprawl, commuter settlements); **example** urban regeneration project.
- Sustainable urban living (water, energy, waste, green space); transport strategies to reduce congestion.

---

#### Section B: The changing economic world (all required)

- Classifying development; measures (GNI, birth/death rates, infant mortality, life expectancy, people per doctor, literacy, safe water, HDI); limitations; DTM link; causes of uneven development (physical, economic, historical); consequences (wealth, health, migration).
- Strategies to reduce development gap (investment, industry, tourism, aid, intermediate technology, fairtrade, debt relief, microfinance); **example** of tourism in LIC/NEE reducing gap.
- **Case study:** one LIC/NEE — location, importance, context, changing industrial structure, sectors, manufacturing, TNCs (advantages/disadvantages), trading relationships, aid (types, impacts), environmental impacts, quality of life.
- UK economic futures: de-industrialisation, globalisation, government policies; post-industrial economy (IT, services, finance, science parks); environmental impacts; **example** sustainable industrial development; rural change (population growth + decline areas); infrastructure (road, rail, ports, airports); north–south divide; UK in wider world (trade, culture, transport, EU, Commonwealth).

---

#### Section C: The challenge of resource management (overview + one choice)

**3.2.3.1 Resource management** (required)
- Significance of food, water, energy; global inequalities in supply/consumption; UK overview (food: high-value exports, food miles, local sourcing, agribusiness; water: demand, quality, pollution, deficit/surplus, transfer; energy: energy mix, fossil fuels, renewables, domestic supplies, economic/environmental issues).

**3.2.3.2 Food** (choose one with 3.2.3.3, 3.2.3.4)
- Surplus/deficit; calorie intake; factors (consumption, supply: climate, technology, pests, water, conflict, poverty); impacts of food insecurity (famine, undernutrition, soil erosion, prices, unrest); strategies (irrigation, aeroponics, hydroponics, biotechnology, appropriate technology); **example** large-scale agricultural development; sustainability (organic, permaculture, urban farming, sustainable fish/meat, seasonal, reduced waste); **example** local scheme in LIC/NEE.

**3.2.3.3 Water** (choose one with 3.2.3.2, 3.2.3.4)
- Surplus/deficit; consumption factors; availability (climate, geology, pollution, over-abstraction, infrastructure, poverty); impacts (waterborne disease, food production, industry, conflict); strategies (diversion, storage, dams, transfers, desalination); **example** large-scale water transfer; sustainability (conservation, groundwater, recycling, grey water); **example** local scheme in LIC/NEE.

**3.2.3.4 Energy** (choose one with 3.2.3.2, 3.2.3.3)
- Surplus/deficit; consumption factors; supply (physical, cost, technology, political); impacts (exploration, costs, food, industry, conflict); strategies (renewable, non-renewable); **example** fossil fuel extraction; sustainability (carbon footprints, energy conservation, design, demand reduction, efficiency); **example** local renewable scheme in LIC/NEE.

### 2.3 Paper 3 — Geographical applications

#### Section A: Issue evaluation
- Pre-release resource booklet (12 weeks before exam); unfamiliar context; synoptic — draws from 3.1 and 3.2; interpret, analyse, evaluate; stakeholders; advantages/disadvantages; reasoned justifications; physical–human interrelationships.

#### Section B: Fieldwork
- Two enquiries; contrasting environments; physical + human; at least one showing physical–human interaction; fieldwork outside classroom on at least two occasions.
- **Enquiry strands:** suitable question; primary/secondary data; measuring/recording; sampling; processing and presenting; describing, analysing, explaining; conclusions; evaluation (problems, limitations, reliability).

## 3. Design Principles for Effective & Engaging Learning

- **Align with AQA:** Every activity maps to spec content and to AO1/AO2/AO3/AO4. Geography-specific: place, scale, interrelationships, sustainability, management.
- **Option-aware:** Students choose Living world (Desert or Cold), Physical landscapes (two from Coastal, River, Glacial), Resource (Food, Water, or Energy). Hub filters content accordingly.
- **Case study depth:** Named examples and case studies are core. Content must support location-specific knowledge (e.g. Rio, Lagos, London, Bristol; one LIC/NEE; coastal scheme; flood scheme; rainforest; desert; cold environment).
- **Processes and landforms:** Physical geography requires understanding of processes (erosion, deposition, weathering) and how they create landforms. Diagrams, animations, and “sequence” activities help.
- **Evaluate and justify:** Paper 3 and extended questions require “evaluate”, “assess”, “to what extent”. Activities should practise stakeholder viewpoints, advantages/disadvantages, reasoned conclusions.
- **Skills integration:** Map, graph, and data skills are assessed in context. Integrate skills practice into topic content, not only as standalone drills.
- **Fieldwork preparation:** Even without real fieldwork, students need enquiry process (question, methods, data, analysis, conclusions, evaluation). Simulated or secondary-data fieldwork tasks.
- **Reuse app patterns:** Mirror Science Lab / History Hub / Business Hub: concept → flashcard → check → questions; progress and gating; config-driven content.

## 5. Learning Modes (Effective & Engaging)

### 5.1 Concept Lab (Learn)

- **Purpose:** Build understanding of processes, interrelationships, and management before recall and exam-style questions.
- **Content per concept:** Core idea (1–3 sentences); diagram where helpful (e.g. plate margins, atmospheric circulation, longshore drift, cross profile); “change scenario” (e.g. “If wave energy increases, how might coastal erosion change?”); common misconception.
- **Format:** Card or short page per concept; link to related topics and flashcards.
- **Coverage:** At least one concept per key idea in spec; more for dense topics (e.g. coastal processes, river processes, development indicators).
- **Engagement:** “Why does this happen?”; “What would change if…?”; visual process diagrams.

### 5.2 Key Terms & Flashcards

- **Purpose:** Secure AO1: definitions, processes, landforms, management strategies, case study facts.
- **Content:** Term → definition; “in context” sentence; link to section/option. Include case study place names and key facts (e.g. “Rio de Janeiro”, “Rochdale”, “Nigeria”, “Holderness”, “Somerset Levels”).
- **Modes:** Classic flip card; “type the term”; “match term–definition”; confidence rating for spaced repetition.
- **Coverage:** Full spec glossary plus case study facts per chosen options.
- **Engagement:** Group by section and option; “Exam tip” for terms that appear in 6/9-mark answers.

### 5.3 Process & Landform Explorer

- **Purpose:** Visualise physical processes and resulting landforms (coastal, river, glacial).
- **Content:** Step-by-step diagrams for formation (e.g. waterfall, meander, wave-cut platform, corrie); labelled cross-sections; “drag to order” sequence (e.g. stages of waterfall formation).
- **Format:** Interactive diagram or carousel; optional annotation task.
- **Coverage:** All landforms in chosen physical landscape options; processes in natural hazards and living world.
- **Engagement:** Visual, spatial; links to Concept Lab and Quick Check.

### 5.4 Quick Check (Micro-Assessments)

- **Purpose:** Check recall and simple application before unlocking extended questions.
- **Formats:** Multiple choice (definition, “which process…”, “which landform…”); True/False; Short answer (e.g. “Name one cause of desertification”); “Which two of the following…”; Drag-and-drop (order formation stages; match landform to process; match management strategy to type).
- **Coverage:** 5–10 items per section per chosen option.
- **Gating:** Unlock Issue Lab and Question Lab when Quick Check passed (e.g. ≥70%).

### 5.5 Skills Lab (Map, Graph, Data)

- **Purpose:** Practise AO4: cartographic, graphical, numerical, statistical skills in context.
- **Content:**  
  - **Map skills:** OS map extracts (or simplified); grid references, scale, direction, relief, land-use; identify features; cross-sections; describe distribution.  
  - **Graph skills:** Interpret choropleth, population pyramid, scatter, line/bar; suggest appropriate graph; complete graph from data.  
  - **Data skills:** Percentage change; mean, median, range; fieldwork-style data (sample size, reliability); trend lines on scatter.
- **Format:** Interactive tasks with feedback; “What does this map/graph show?”; “Suggest the best way to present…”.
- **Coverage:** Skills from 3.4 applied to topics from 3.1 and 3.2; at least 2–3 tasks per skill area.
- **Engagement:** Real-world contexts; link to case study areas where possible.

### 5.6 Issue Lab (Paper 3 — Issue Evaluation)

- **Purpose:** Practise synoptic evaluation: interpret resources, consider stakeholders, evaluate options, justify decisions.
- **Content:** Resource booklet–style materials (maps, graphs, photos, quotes) on a geographical issue; structured questions: “What does Source A show?”; “Outline one advantage of Option X”; “Evaluate the options”; “Which option do you support? Justify.”
- **Format:** Multi-source view; question-by-question with model answers and mark-scheme breakdown.
- **Coverage:** 3–5 issue scenarios drawing from different spec themes; updated annually when pre-release available (note: pre-release is exam-specific; generic issue scenarios for general practice).
- **Engagement:** Real contemporary issues; stakeholder role-play; “What would you decide?”.

### 5.7 Fieldwork Lab (Paper 3 — Fieldwork)

- **Purpose:** Practise enquiry process: question, methods, data collection, presentation, analysis, conclusions, evaluation.
- **Content:**  
  - **Unfamiliar context:** Given fieldwork data/results from an unknown location; questions on methods, presentation, analysis, conclusions, evaluation.  
  - **Own enquiry:** Template for students to record their two enquiries (titles, locations, methods, findings); practice questions on “your enquiry”.
- **Format:** Data sets + questions; template for recording own fieldwork.
- **Coverage:** Physical and human enquiry examples; contrasting environments; enquiry strands 1–6.
- **Engagement:** “Design an enquiry for…”; “What could have improved this enquiry?”.

### 5.8 Question Lab (Extended Response)

- **Purpose:** Exam-style AO1+AO2+AO3: describe, explain, assess, evaluate.
- **Content:**  
  - **Describe:** “Describe the distribution of…”; “Describe two features of…”.  
  - **Explain:** “Explain how…”; “Explain why…”.  
  - **Assess/Evaluate:** “Assess the effectiveness of…”; “Evaluate the impacts of…”; “To what extent…”.  
  - **Case study:** “Use a case study to explain…”; “Using a named example…”.
- **Format:** Structured or free text with word limit; model answer and mark-scheme breakdown (knowledge, understanding, application, evaluation).
- **Coverage:** At least one of each question type per section for chosen options; case study prompts for all required case studies.
- **Engagement:** SPaG reminder for extended responses; “Method mark” style feedback (idea + knowledge + evaluation).

### 5.9 Topic Overview & Revision Map

- **Purpose:** Show coverage at a glance; “your options” and “what’s next” per section.
- **Content:** For each section and chosen option: list of topics with status (Concept, Terms, Quick Check, Skills, Issue Lab, Fieldwork, Question Lab). Progress bars per paper.
- **Engagement:** “Recommended next”; “Complete Quick Check for Coastal to unlock Question Lab.”

## 9. Engagement Tactics (Summary)

- **Place and scale:** Emphasise “where” and “at what scale” in every activity; link to real locations.
- **Process before landform:** Understand erosion/deposition/weathering before naming landforms.
- **Case study ownership:** “Your case studies” — LIC/NEE city, UK city, LIC/NEE country, coastal scheme, etc. — visible and practised repeatedly.
- **Stakeholder thinking:** Issue evaluation and management questions — “Who benefits? Who loses?” — practised in Issue Lab and Question Lab.
- **Visual and spatial:** Maps, diagrams, cross-sections, process sequences; interactive where possible.
- **Skills in context:** Map and graph tasks use topic data (e.g. population of Rio, rainfall for Somerset).
- **Clear path:** “Concept → Terms → Quick Check → Skills / Issue / Fieldwork / Question Lab” visible; gating so harder tasks unlock after basics.
- **Varied formats:** Mix concept cards, flashcards, MC, drag-and-drop, diagram annotation, data interpretation, extended writing to maintain interest.
- **SPaG and terminology:** Remind on extended responses; specialist terms practised in flashcards.
